Module 1: Telephony Basics

  1. A brief history

    Alexander Graham Bell applied for the patent in 1876. Westenr Union found it of no practical use and didn't purchase it.

    By 1877 he gut funding for the Bell Telephone Company and by 1878 the company was licensec to provide telephony in USA.

    In 1900 AT&T became the parent company of the Bell System. AT&T wanted to manage the long distance business of the Bell licencees.

    After Bell's patents expired in 1893 and 1894, numerous independent companies (over 6000) offered telephony services. Their networks were not interconnected, so users had to have different telephones for different purposes.

    This initialy shaped the networks. To communicate two telephones from two different Bell companies,additional long-distance facilities were required. This is the structure of the public switch telephone system (PSTN).

    Eventually AT&T started purchasing independent companies to alleviate competition and increase revenue.

    1. Kingsbury Commitment

      In 1913 the US DOJ brought a monopoly suit against AT&T forcing it to stop purchasing smaller telcos. This Kingsbury Commitment provided:

      • An arrengement to provide long distance to the independents,
      • Bell's agreement to stop buying independent telcos,
      • the end of the duplication of the telephone service, which finally brought
      • the creation of the best national telecommunications facilities worldwide.

  3. Transmission network

    MCI's is all digital. Signals are processed in order to place several in a wire (copper, coaxial, fiber optic or radio signal).

    Multiplexing: combining several signal in one is called multiplexing.

    One form of multiplexor "Channel Bank" takes several different signals (analog or digital) and does:

    • converts analog voice into a specific format of digital,
    • formats all digital signals to a specific format, and
    • combines all the digital signals into a single one.

  4. Circuits

    The physical connection (or path) between two given points through with an electric current may be established.

    1. End-To-End circuit: A source device connecting to an alike destination device. For example one telephone reaching another to complete the circuit. Both process the same type of analog signal.
    2. Carrier circuit: A circuit that comines (multiplexes) several signals and moves then from one place to another. It is connected to other carrier circuits at junctions within the network. The network can be defined as local or long-distance.

  5. Digital signals

    Digital signals are those regarded as communicating bits.

  6. The digital signal hierarchy

    DS-0 (Digital Signal zer0-level) is the base circuit capacity, at 64,000 bits per second. It is designed to carry a voice communication made of an eight-bit signal sampled 8,000 times per second, hence 8 x 8000 = 64000 bandwidth.

    In the hierarchy, the combination of 24 DS-0s makes a DS-1 at 24 x 64000 = 1,536,000 bps plus the framing bits giving 1,544,000bps.

    1. Time Division Multiplexing (TDM)

      The multiplexer transmits 8 bits of each DS-0, when it reaches the last one it starts over with slot one. This happens 8000 times per second, the sampling frequency of each DS-0. A framing bit is added after each cycle, so the total bandwidth is 8000 + 1,536,000 bps.

    2. Digital Signal One (DS-1)

      The DS-1 circuit is the first highway (or carrier circuit) onto the network. Is like a 24-lane highway if each DS-0 is a single road.

      TDM combines four DS-1 to make a DS-2 (not used) and seven DS-2 to make a DS-3 signal. A DS-3 can be made aldo of 28 DS-1s bypassing the Ds-2 level.

  7. Aggregating Bandwidth

    A 256Kbps circuit can be made by aggregating 4 DS-0 channels.

    A DS-3 can be fractionalized in numbers of DS-1 channels.

    Often signals don't need to be regarded as channels, the user wants a single channel providing all the aggregate bandwith.

  8. Fiber Optics

    A fiber optic cable is a core inside a cladding, both made of slightly different ultra-pure glass.

    These two layers are contained into a protective plastic coating, usually made of a soft cushion enclosed in a tougher outer layer. .

    Light pulses are injected in one tip of the cable using a laser or a LED. The light is totally reflected inside the core by the surface between the core and the cladding, in a way that allows the light signals to reach distances like 100 miles even if the cable to be bended.

    Light signals carry much more information than electrical signals because of the higher frequency of the light waves.

    1. Electrical to Optical Signal Conversion

      These devices receive electric digital impulses and retransmit them as light signals, or receive light pulses and convert them to electric digital signals.

  9. Synchronous Optical NETwork (SONET)

    SONET is a transmission standard (a protocol) for fiber optic systems.

    It defines line rates known as Optical Carrier (OC) units.

    The OC-1 base rate provides 51.84Mbps, close to a DS-3 signal. IXC backbone netxorps operate at OC-48 or OC-192 speeds. OC-192 is a network line with transmission speeds of up to 9953.28 Mbit/s (payload: 9621.504 Mbit/s; overhead: 331.776 Mbit/s).

Module 5: Private line fundamentals

Private Line is a category of dedicated, point-to-point, digital communications services. May be local, long-distance or international.

Types can be

  • DS-0 voice

  • analof data and digital (DDS)

  • Full and fractional DS-1

  • Full and fractional DS-3

  • Optical Carrier OC-3 and OC-12 circuits.

  1. Private line circuit elements

    • Local access
      • customer premise
      • C.O.
      • C.O.
    • IXC
      • MCI POP
      • MCI POP
    • Local access
      • C.O.
      • C.O.
      • customer premise

    The characteristic is that the circuit is dedicated to the customer ("pinned") and not switched.

    The elements required to build the circuit are the IXC and Local Access (in both tips).

     

    1. Interexcange Carrier (IXC) Element

      It's the part between the MCI POP locations on MCI long distance networks. Sometimes referred as IMTs (inter-machine trunks).

    2. Local Access Element

      Is the circuit from the POPs to the customer premises.

      It consists of two CFAs (Carrier Facility Assignments), one between the customer premise and the LEC (Local Exchange Carrier) central office and one between the MCI POP and the LEC central office.

      The CFA is the interface between network types, like between LEC and IXC, or CLEC (Competitive LEC) and a LEC, or between two IXCs.

      The LEC CO serving the POP might be the same on both locations.

  2. DS-0 private line services

    1. DS-0 voice (VF)

      Commonly used with VF (voice frequency) to support voice applications like:

      • OPX (off-premise extensions),
      • tie lines,
      • ringdown, and
      • shout-down / hoot-n-roller circuits.
    2. Off premise extensiond (OPX)

      An OPX is a two-wire circuit extenden from a PBX to a station somewhere else. The OPX functions as an extension of the PBX.

    3. Tie Line

      A connection between two PBXs as a trunk. Normally terminated as four wires at the customer location. The billing is not usage rated, so the customer can do as many call as desired, only one at a time per DS-0.

    4. Ringdown

      Connects a pair of phones, each one rings when the other's handset is picked up.

      There are two types:

      Automatic Ringdown (ARD)

      The ARD operates when the telephone handset is picked up in one terminal ans causes a ring at the other one.

      Manual Ringdown

      In addition to picking the handset, the caller part has to press a button. These are common in the financial services industry and the military.

    5. Shoutdown / Hoot N' Holler

      The line is open all times and connected to a speaker and a microphone at each end.

    6. DS-0 Analog Data

      The service supports frequencies in the range 300 ~ 3000Hz. Data transmission speeds range from 2.4 through 56Kbps, used for Internet connections.

      Analog data circuits can be used to connect faxes or modems at the customer's premises.

      Analog circuits come with 2 or 4 wires. A 4-wire circuit can transmit and receive at the same time. There are modems that can do duplex over 2-wire channels.

    7. DS-0 Digital Data Services (DDS)

      Four-wire circuits supporting data transmissión at 2.4, 4.8, 9.6, 56 or 64 Kbps.

      Typically used in applications requiring low bit error rate and high speed transmission.

      The key is end-to-end digital transmission. The result is maximun quality with a minimum of distortion.

      The most common application is a line between the customer system and a remote DTE (Digital Terminal Equipment).

    8. DS-0 Multi-Point Circuit

      Connects three or more locations. The applications can be DDS or analog.

      It is a single channel shared among several stations that individually do not generate enough traffic to justify full-time point-to-point circuits.

      The circuits are multi-drop (terminated in the same city) or multi-point (terminate drops in different cities).

  • Network diversity

    A frugal network design, with no contingency options, might initially seem less expensive but after having an outage, in might prove to be much less convenient.

    To combate outages there are three options:

    • Order duplicate facilities from another carrier. This is the most common approach.

    • Order duplicate or redundant circuits from the same carrier at a recuced rate. It is desirable that those different circuits use different routings to enhance survability.

    • There are other diversity options:

      • Optical diversity: The circuits are provisiones on different OC facilities.
      • Local diversity: The local accesses are ordered (via CFA) to different central offices (LEC and MCI local).
      • DCS diversity: The customer circuits are provisioned to alternate DCS.
      • DS-3 diversity: The customer circuits are provisiones to alternate DS-3 circiots.
